Sir Noel Short

Sir Noel Short, who has died aged 85, was Secretary to three Speakers of the House of Commons in the 1970s and early 1980s; earlier, as a Gurkha officer, he had won an MC fighting the Japanese in the jungles of Burma.
12 July 2001 • 12:00am
Short joined the House in 1970 from the Home Office, where he had been a Principal in the Police Department since retiring from the Army in the rank of brigadier six years before. In the Commons, he worked first for Horace King, then for Selwyn Lloyd and finally for George Thomas.
His duties were wide and varied, ranging from organising translation services for the Speaker when he met foreign visitors, to helping him memorise the names of new MPs after a general election. Short kept an album with photographs of these close at hand in the chamber so that Speaker could familiarise himself with their faces during any lull in proceedings. Should his recall fail him, Short was always ready with a prompt.
He had spent much of his 28 years in the Army in the Far East and spoke four languages fluently. He accompanied the Speakers on visits abroad, where his natural air of authority occasionally caused confusion among the locals as to who was the senior dignitary.
On one such occasion in Tokyo, having studiously ignored well-meant but frustratingly persistent questions as to whether he had previously met any Japanese, his diplomacy temporarily deserted him. "I got to know them quite well in 1945," came his testy response. His hosts' attention quickly returned to Mr Speaker.
Noel Edward Vivian Short was born at Henley-on-Thames, Oxfordshire, on January 19 1916. His father and grandfather were senior figures in the Indian Police, and he spent part of his childhood in India.
He was educated at Radley and then at Sandhurst, from where he was commissioned into the Indian Army in 1936. His first posting was to the North West Frontier with the South Wales Border Regiment. A year later he joined the 6th Gurkha Rifles, with whom he served for the rest of his military career, and to whom he always maintained a close attachment.
During the Second World War, he saw active service in New Guinea, Assam and Burma. By March 1945 he was at Tongyl, Burma, where he won the Military Cross for his part in an action which, for him, was given an edge by his desire to avenge enemy acts of particular savagery against local inhabitants which he had witnessed.
Short, then a major, was in command of a battalion of the 6th Gurkha Rifles ordered to attack a Japanese force entrenched in bunkers concealed in the jungle and in houses in a large village. Heavy machine-gun fire and snipers hidden in the tree tops held up the Gurkhas, and when tanks arrived to support they were also held up by accurate fire from a 75 mm gun and mortars.
The Gurkhas' casualties mounted steadily, but Short moved from place to place, encouraging the men and co-ordinating the further combined infantry and tank moves required. Largely due to his determination and indifference to danger, the attack was once more resumed and took all its objectives.
At Maymyo nine days later, Short boldly advanced his battalion very rapidly over difficult country, surprising the enemy and overcoming all attempts by them to make a stand. "Major Short's personal courage, dash and leadership," stated the citation for his MC, "have been a great inspiration to his men."
From 1950 to 1951, Short served in Malaya during the Emergency, and in the latter year was appointed MBE. He was promoted to command 63 Gurkha Infantry Brigade in 1960, and two years later 51 Infantry Brigade, based at Tidworth. In 1964, however, with his three children growing up, he left the Army for the greater stability of life in the Civil Service.
He became Colonel of the 6th Queen Elizabeth's Own Gurkha Rifles in 1978, and took a close interest in the welfare of the officers and men thereafter. Away from his desk, he particularly enjoyed improving his skills as a photographer.
Noel Short was knighted in 1977, five years before he retired from working for the Speaker. A modest man, who commanded respect but did not stand on ceremony, he disapproved of any tendency to dwell on Britain's past military glories. He regarded the principles for which he had fought as more important than the fighting itself. He attributed his knighthood to being a reward for "40 years of undetected delinquency".
He married first, in 1949, Diana Morison. She died in 1951, and he married secondly, in 1957, Karin Anders. She survives him, together with his son of the first marriage and a son and a daughter of the second.
